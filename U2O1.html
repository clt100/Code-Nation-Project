1.5

Copyright is a massive issue when it comes to online media, anyone with a little bit of knowledge can take text, images, videos and in some cases, website ideas/designs and modify them to be used in there own project.

Another thing that companies have to consider when creating sites is making sure they have the right licences for the technologies used to build the website, because if they dont, then that is classed as illegal.

GDPR also plays a massive role here, but I've explained that in an earlier answer and won't be covering it here.

What I will be covering though, is in the past it was fairly easy to get away with plagiarism, in recent years there's really been a crackdown on stolen content, either from the owner of the content or third-party companies looking out for this.

With a lot of websites now allowing people people to post any kind of media, it now also falls on the heads of the site owners as well as the person posting, but there is a way for companies to opt out of being responsible.

Knowing this, the control is more so in the users hands than the owners these days, so companies will right out terms and conditions were they will state that any plagiarised content or content deemed not suitable for the website can and will be taken down with the addition that there account may also be banned or removed from the website entirely.

This means that companies have to hire someone to write out these terms and conditions, along with implementing a way to execute on these terms and conditions.

This used to mean that they would need to hire people to purely scan their websites 24/7 and remove stuff, but with recent technological advancements companies are now able to hire developers to design algorithms to do this job for them.

An algorithm is basically an AI piece of software that follows specific guidelines and automatically produces outputs that a human would do, meaning a complete 100% reduction in people hired to purely scan through and remove posts/users from the companies site that are not welcome.

1.6

The easiest accessibility issue to address, when it comes to web-pages, is people with poor sight, which is an easy issue to fix as increasing the size of the text, increasing contrast and showing appropriate alt text for images. This is a fairly simple fix that many websites have as an option.

There's also another big issue with web-page accessibility that effects both visually impaired and non-visually impaired people the same. This issue is very common on websites and consists of too much text or too many links in a given space, which is often seen in the navigation section and/or footer section of a website. The easiest fix is good UX/UI design which splits the text/links up and is labelled in an appropriate and competent way.

Here's just a couple of examples, but when it comes to website accessibility, it many comes down to the restrictions of the users ability to use a computer as a whole, as apposed to there ability to use the website alone.
