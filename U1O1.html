1.7

GDPR was introduced in 2018 due to the massive amounts of peoples private data being stored on company servers without any regulation of how this data can be used or interacted with.

A lot of companies would have this data and send it to other companies, usually for a payment, without the users permissions, which would lead to these third-party companies contacting the users and targeting them for marketing campaigns (commonly known as spam emails).

Along with this, there were no regulations in place that allowed the user to determine whether these companies should even have there data stored and on the flip side there was no law stating that the company had to truthfully share what data they had linked to the user.

With the introduction of GDPR all Europian companies had to change their data policies to allign with this new law over the period of a few months.

The way that this impacted companies intellectual properties, was by forcing them to add definitively clear to see messages that usually links the user to a page explaining exactly how there data is stored, updated and used along with how the user can contact the company to request either a physical copy of there data or to remove their data all together.

This also impacted the way that data can be passed from one company to another, as the user would now be presented with exactly where their data was going and how it was going to be used, followed by the option to opt-in to allowing their data to be passed over to the corrosponding company.

Although data flow still works exactly the same, as it did before GDPR (with a few extra steps), the main differences are that the user can now understand how data flow works and opt-out of any personal data being transfered to somone or somewhere that they dont want to have access to their information.
